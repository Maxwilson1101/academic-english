# Argument Genre Specification

## Scope

Argument genres justify a claim about a debatable issue by using reasons, evidence, evaluation, and critical comparison.

Use an argument genre when the task requires defending one position, weighing competing positions, resolving a dispute, or making an issue more certain through reasoning.

For language templates that realize these structures, use [Argument Expression Patterns](argument-expressions.md).

Do not use an argument genre when the task primarily requires static description, classification, part-whole composition, causal explanation, instructions, procedural reporting, or storytelling.

## General Rules

- Keep the text reasoned, evidence-based, and issue-oriented.
- Organize content by claims, reasons, evidence, positions, and resolution.
- Use explicit argumentative signals when the relation between claims, reasons, evidence, objections, and conclusions could be missed.
- Begin by identifying the debatable issue, topic, or problem.
- Make stage boundaries clear through paragraphing, headings, topic sentences, or another visible structure.
- Treat each major reason, supporting argument, opposing position, or resolution point as a distinct phase when the text is long enough to require structure.
- Support claims with reasons, examples, evidence, principles, or logical explanation.
- Address opposing views when the genre type or communicative purpose requires comparison or rebuttal.
- Do not present a sequence of actions as commands; use a procedural genre for instructions.
- Do not organize primarily by causes and effects unless the causal reasoning supports an argument; use an explanation genre for causal accounts.

## Genre Types

### Exposition

**Purpose:** Argue for one point of view on a debatable issue.

**Use when:** The writer takes a clear position and mainly develops reasons and evidence in support of that position.

**Required stages:**

1. **Thesis**
   - Introduce the topic or debatable issue.
   - Present the writer's position on the issue.
   - Preview the main reasons for the position when useful.
   - Provide essential background when it helps readers understand the issue.

2. **Arguments**
   - Elaborate the reasons in favor of the thesis.
   - Support each reason with evidence, examples, principles, explanation, or logical reasoning.
   - Present each major argument as a distinct phase or paragraph when there is enough detail.
   - Mention and refute an opposing view only when doing so strengthens the thesis.

3. **Reiteration**
   - Restate the thesis.
   - Summarize or reinforce the main reasons when useful.
   - End by emphasizing the significance, consequence, warning, or implication of the position.

**Structure rules:**

- The Thesis stage must precede the Arguments stage.
- The Arguments stage must support the position stated in the Thesis stage.
- Each argument phase should begin with a clear topic sentence or claim when the text is long enough to require structure.
- Reasons should be logically connected to the thesis rather than listed without explanation.
- Opposing views are optional and should not become the main organizing principle.
- Do not give balanced treatment to competing positions as the main structure; use a discussion for that.

### Discussion

**Purpose:** Weigh two or more points of view on a debatable issue and resolve the debate in favor of one position.

**Use when:** The task requires examining competing positions before reaching or presenting a reasoned resolution.

**Required stages:**

1. **Issue**
   - Introduce the debatable issue.
   - Define key terms when needed.
   - Preview the different positions concerning the issue.
   - Avoid taking a firm position at the beginning unless the expected format requires it.

2. **Sides**
   - Present the competing positions one by one.
   - Begin each side phase with a topic sentence that states the position.
   - Provide reasons and evidence in support of each position.
   - Give each major position an appropriate amount of development.
   - Make contrasts between positions clear when needed.

3. **Resolution**
   - Review the competing positions.
   - Resolve the debate in favor of one position or a qualified position.
   - Explain why the selected position is stronger, more reasonable, or more appropriate.
   - State recommendations, conditions, limits, or safeguards when useful.

**Structure rules:**

- The Issue stage must precede the Sides and Resolution stages.
- The Sides stage must present at least two positions on the issue.
- Each major position should be developed in a separate paragraph or clearly marked phase when there is enough detail.
- Evidence should be provided for each position, not only for the final preferred position.
- The Resolution stage must connect back to the issue and the positions discussed.
- Do not focus only on one side from the beginning; use an exposition for that.

## Genre Selection

- Use **exposition** for one position supported by reasons and evidence.
- Use **discussion** for competing positions weighed before a resolution.
- If a topic supports more than one argumentative relation, choose the genre matching the primary task:
  - one defended viewpoint -> exposition
  - two or more competing viewpoints -> discussion

## Output Contract

A valid argument must:

- Declare or imply exactly one primary organizing relation: one-sided support or competing-position evaluation.
- Include all required stages for the selected genre.
- Maintain consistent terminology for the issue, thesis, positions, reasons, evidence, and resolution.
- Use phases to separate major reasons, arguments, sides, or resolution points.
- Provide enough reasoning or evidence for each major claim to explain its relevance to the argument.
- Preserve the relation between thesis and supporting arguments in expositions.
- Preserve the relation between issue, sides, and resolution in discussions.
- Make the final position or resolution clear.
