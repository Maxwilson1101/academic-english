# Explanation Expression Patterns

## Scope

Explanation expression patterns provide reusable wording for realizing causal meanings in explanation genres.

Use this file when generating or revising language inside an explanation. For genre purposes, required stages, and selection rules, use [Explanation Genre Specification](explanation.md).

Replace `cause`, `effect`, `phenomenon`, `condition`, `A`, `B`, `X`, and `Y` with topic-specific content.

## General Rules

- Select expressions according to the causal relation being explained: sequence, multiple causes, multiple effects, or condition-result variation.
- Use causal conjunctions when linking a cause clause to an effect clause.
- Use causal verbs when making the cause-effect relation the main predicate of the sentence.
- Use causal nouns when naming causes, reasons, factors, consequences, or influences.
- Use causal prepositional phrases when giving a compact reason for an outcome.
- Use causal adverbs and adverbial phrases when connecting one sentence or clause to a resulting effect.
- Keep causal direction clear: do not confuse `cause -> effect` with `effect -> cause`.
- Use cautious modality such as `may`, `can`, or `is associated with` when the causal relation is possible, indirect, or not fully certain.

## Causal Conjunction Expressions

Use these patterns to connect a cause clause with an effect clause.

- `Effect occurs because cause.`
- `Effect occurs as cause.`
- `Effect occurs since cause.`
- `Effect occurs while condition remains true.`
- `Seeing that cause, effect must be addressed.`
- `Now that condition has occurred, effect follows.`

Examples:

- `We had to put the meeting off because so many people were absent.`
- `You will never save any money while you are so extravagant.`
- `Seeing that the project engages several sectors, risks of weak linkages have to be addressed.`
- `Now that students have been admitted to college, they should complete the enrollment process.`

## Causal Verb Expressions

Use these patterns when the causal relation is expressed through the main verb.

### Cause To Effect

- `Cause may cause effect.`
- `Cause may give rise to effect.`
- `Cause can lead to effect.`
- `Cause can result in effect.`
- `X may affect Y.`
- `X may be associated with Y.`

### Effect To Cause

- `Effect is caused by cause.`
- `Effect results from cause.`
- `Effect stems from cause.`

Examples:

- `Lack of protein may cause mental retardation.`
- `Lack of protein can lead to mental retardation.`
- `Kwashiorkor is a disease caused by insufficient protein.`
- `There is some evidence that X may affect Y.`
- `Stomach cancer in many cases may be associated with certain bacterial infections.`

## Causal Noun Expressions

Use these patterns when the cause-effect relation is expressed through nouns such as `cause`, `factor`, `reason`, `influence`, or `consequence`.

- `The main cause of effect is cause.`
- `The two main causes of effect are cause and cause.`
- `Cause is an important factor in effect.`
- `Another reason why phenomenon occurs is that cause.`
- `X has an influence on Y.`
- `A consequence of cause is effect.`
- `One possible consequence of phenomenon is effect.`

Examples:

- `The two main causes of inflation are demand-pull and cost-push.`
- `Physical activity is an important factor in maintaining fitness.`
- `Another reason why printed books are considered important is that they provide a sense of touch.`
- `Many medications have an influence on cholesterol levels.`
- `A consequence of vitamin A deficiency is blindness.`

## Causal Prepositional Phrase Expressions

Use these patterns when giving a compact cause after an effect.

- `Effect occurs owing to cause.`
- `Effect occurs because of cause.`
- `Effect occurs on account of cause.`
- `Effect occurs as a result of cause.`
- `Effect occurs due to cause.`

Example:

- `Many people become deaf because of a lack of iodine.`
- `Many people become deaf as a result of a lack of iodine.`
- `Many people become deaf due to a lack of iodine.`

## Causal Adverb And Adverbial Expressions

Use these patterns when connecting a previous cause or situation to a following effect.

- `Cause occurs. Therefore, effect follows.`
- `Cause occurs. Consequently, effect follows.`
- `Cause occurs. Accordingly, effect follows.`
- `Cause occurs. Subsequently, effect follows.`
- `Cause occurs. Because of this, effect follows.`
- `Cause occurs. As a result, effect follows.`
- `Cause occurs, thus producing effect.`
- `Cause occurs, thereby producing effect.`
- `Cause occurs, hence producing effect.`

Examples:

- `Undernourished children tend to have decreased learning ability. Therefore, when they grow up, it may be difficult for them to find work.`
- `The warm air rises above the surface of the sea, thereby creating an area of low pressure.`

## Evaluation Checks

- Does the expression match the selected [explanation genre](explanation.md#genre-types)?
- Is the causal direction clear?
- Are placeholders replaced with specific, domain-appropriate content?
- Does the expression use appropriate certainty or caution?
- Are causes, effects, conditions, and results named consistently?
- Does the expression preserve the primary causal organization of the explanation?
