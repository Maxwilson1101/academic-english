# Explanation Genre Specification

## Scope

Explanation genres explain how or why a phenomenon happens by organizing causes, effects, events, or conditions into a causal account.

Use an explanation genre when the task requires analysis of causal relations in natural, social, technical, or scientific phenomena.

For language templates that realize these structures, use [Explanation Expression Patterns](explanation-expressions.md).

Do not use an explanation genre when the task primarily requires static description, classification, or part-whole composition; use [Report Genre Specification](report.md) for those purposes.

## General Rules

- Keep the text objective, informational, and cause-oriented.
- Organize content by causal relations, not by personal experience, argument, or instructions.
- Use timeless present tense for general processes and factual causal relations unless the subject requires another tense.
- Begin by identifying the phenomenon to be explained.
- Make stage boundaries clear through paragraphing, headings, topic sentences, or another visible structure.
- Treat each major event, cause, effect, or condition-result pairing as a distinct phase when the text is long enough to require structure.
- Use explicit causal signals when the relation between events, causes, effects, or conditions could be missed.
- Do not present a sequence of actions as commands; use a procedure genre for instructions.
- Do not present past events as a personal or historical record; use a recount or narrative genre for event records or stories.

## Core Stages

All explanation genres require two stages.

1. **Phenomenon**
   - Identify the phenomenon to be explained.
   - Define the phenomenon when needed.
   - Provide essential background, scope, or context.
   - Establish the causal relation to be explained.

2. **Explanation**
   - Explain how or why the phenomenon happens.
   - Present relevant events, causes, effects, or conditions.
   - Preserve the causal logic required by the selected explanation type.

**Optional stage:**

3. **Definition**
   - Define the phenomenon before or after the Explanation stage.
   - Use only when a concise definition improves clarity.

## Causal Signalling

### Explicit Explanation

**Definition:** A causal explanation that states causal relations with overt linguistic signals.

**Common signals:** `because`, `so`, `therefore`, `lead to`, `is caused by`, `as a result`, `contribute to`, `result in`, `depend on`, `if`, `when`.

**Use when:** The causal relation may be complex, non-obvious, or important for reader comprehension.

### Implicit Explanation

**Definition:** A causal explanation that leaves causal relations to be inferred from sequence, context, or semantic association.

**Use when:** The causal relation is simple, conventional, and easy to infer.

**Constraint:** Do not rely on implicit causality if ambiguity is likely.

## Genre Types

### Sequential Explanation

**Purpose:** Explain a phenomenon produced by a chronological sequence of causally connected events.

**Use when:** The target phenomenon is a process, operation, cycle, or mechanism that unfolds through ordered phases.

**Required stages:**

1. **Phenomenon**
   - Identify the process or mechanism to be explained.
   - Name important participants, organs, parts, materials, or systems involved.
   - Define the process when useful.

2. **Explanation**
   - Present events in chronological order.
   - Show how each event leads to or enables the next event.
   - Use one phase for each major event or process step.
   - Conclude with the final state, result, or completed cycle when relevant.

**Structure rules:**

- The Phenomenon stage must precede the Explanation stage.
- The Explanation stage must preserve event order.
- Each phase must connect causally to the preceding or following phase.
- Use present tense for general processes.
- Do not frame phases as instructions to the reader; that would make the text procedural.

### Factorial Explanation

**Purpose:** Explain a phenomenon by identifying multiple causes that contribute to one outcome.

**Use when:** The target phenomenon results from several causes or factors.

**Required stages:**

1. **Phenomenon**
   - Identify the outcome or phenomenon to be explained.
   - Define the phenomenon when needed.
   - State that multiple causes or contributing factors are involved.

2. **Explanation**
   - Identify each contributing cause or factor.
   - Explain how each factor contributes to the outcome.
   - Present each major factor as a distinct phase when there is enough detail.

**Structure rules:**

- The Phenomenon stage must precede the Explanation stage.
- Causes are parallel contributors to the same outcome.
- Do not imply a chronological chain between causes unless one is actually being explained.
- Use causal signals such as `contribute to`, `is caused by`, `results from`, and `because` when needed.
- Do not organize primarily by effects; use a consequential explanation for that.

### Consequential Explanation

**Purpose:** Explain the multiple effects or consequences produced by one cause or phenomenon.

**Use when:** The target phenomenon causes several outcomes.

**Required stages:**

1. **Phenomenon**
   - Identify the cause or phenomenon producing consequences.
   - Define the phenomenon when needed.
   - State that it produces multiple effects.

2. **Explanation**
   - Identify each major effect or consequence.
   - Explain how each effect follows from the phenomenon.
   - Group effects into meaningful phases when useful, such as short-term and long-term consequences.

**Structure rules:**

- The Phenomenon stage must precede the Explanation stage.
- Effects are parallel outcomes of the same cause unless a secondary causal chain is explicitly stated.
- Use causal signals such as `leads to`, `results in`, `causes`, `as a result`, and `therefore` when needed.
- Do not organize primarily by contributing causes; use a factorial explanation for that.

### Conditional Explanation

**Purpose:** Explain how different conditions produce different effects.

**Use when:** The result varies depending on condition, context, state, threshold, or case.

**Required stages:**

1. **Phenomenon**
   - Identify the phenomenon whose effects vary.
   - Define key concepts when needed.
   - State the variable condition that controls the result.

2. **Explanation**
   - Identify each relevant condition.
   - State the effect that occurs under each condition.
   - Explain the relation between condition and effect.
   - Present each condition-result pairing as a distinct phase when there is enough detail.

**Structure rules:**

- The Phenomenon stage must precede the Explanation stage.
- Each condition must be paired with a corresponding effect.
- Use explicit conditional signals such as `if`, `when`, `depending on`, `under this condition`, and `otherwise`.
- Do not merge condition-result pairings if the results differ.
- Do not present different results as unrelated effects; preserve the controlling condition for each result.

## Genre Selection

- Use **sequential explanation** for one process explained through ordered causal events.
- Use **factorial explanation** for one outcome explained by multiple causes.
- Use **consequential explanation** for one cause or phenomenon explained through multiple effects.
- Use **conditional explanation** for different effects controlled by different conditions.
- If a topic supports more than one causal relation, choose the genre matching the primary task:
  - ordered events -> sequential explanation
  - multiple causes -> factorial explanation
  - multiple effects -> consequential explanation
  - variable conditions -> conditional explanation

## Output Contract

A valid explanation must:

- Declare or imply exactly one primary causal organization: sequence, multiple causes, multiple effects, or condition-result variation.
- Include the Phenomenon stage and the Explanation stage.
- Define the phenomenon when the target concept may be unfamiliar or technical.
- Maintain consistent terminology for the phenomenon, causes, effects, events, conditions, and results.
- Use phases to separate major causal units.
- Make causal relations explicit when inference would be unreliable.
- Preserve the causal logic of the selected explanation type throughout the text.
